About This Tool

What it is, who it's for, and what it produces

This is an interactive facilitation aid for People & Culture Strategy Partners working through organizational design engagements using the AlignOrg framework. It is designed to be run on the facilitator's screen — or projected as a shared working surface — to capture decisions and guide the group through a structured design process.

How the Tool Works

Three steps from context to recommendations to decisions

1 Complete Core Macro Steps Gap Analysis → Design Criteria → Organizing Rationale → Linkages
2 Visit Recommended Path The tool analyses your inputs and tells you which additional tools are worth your time
3 Work Recommended Sections Use only what's relevant — not every section needs to be completed

The Diagnostic Framework + Recommended Path work together

  • Complete the Diagnostic Framework (under Session) before the group session — it generates warning flags and focus areas that feed directly into Recommended Path
  • Recommended Path (after Linkages in the sidebar) is your dynamic session agenda — it updates live as you enter data
  • Amber dots (●) appear next to strongly recommended sections in the sidebar so you never have to leave a section to check recommendations

Before Your Session

Facilitator preparation checklist

Facilitator Note

Work through this checklist before participants join. The diagnostic and recommended path should be reviewed in advance so you arrive with a proposed session agenda.
  • Complete Session Setup — org name, scope, objectives, and design constraints
  • Run through the Diagnostic Framework — answer all 7 questions and review the generated flags
  • Review Recommended Path — use it to build your session agenda and decide which sections to prioritize
  • Enable Facilitator Mode and read the facilitator notes for each section you plan to use
  • If continuing a prior session — Import JSON from the Session Summary section to restore previous data
  • Confirm the design scope with the sponsor — what is and is not available for redesign

During Your Session

Facilitation tips for running effective OD sessions with this tool

  • Follow the core steps in order (Gap Analysis → Design Criteria → Organizing Rationale → Linkages) before moving to situational tools — each step informs the next
  • Share the Recommended Path with participants to align the group on which additional tools you will and won't be using in this session
  • Use the guide-box questions in each section to drive discussion — the tool captures decisions and outputs, not verbatim conversation
  • Facilitator notes (visible in Facilitator Mode) are for the P&C partner only — disable Facilitator Mode before projecting to participants
  • The RACI table, Design Options, and Action Plan are most powerful at the end of a session — use them to close out decisions and assign ownership before participants leave the room
  • Use JSON Export at the end of each session to create a checkpoint — do this before clearing or closing the browser
  • If the group gets stuck, return to Gap Analysis — most design disagreements trace back to unresolved gaps in desired results

Session Setup

Capture the context for this organizational design engagement

Facilitator Note

Complete this before the session begins. Share the Org Name and Scope with participants to align expectations. The Objectives field should capture the specific outcomes this group is trying to achieve through this OD work.
Tip Agree on design scope early. Misalignment on what IS and IS NOT in scope for redesign is one of the most common causes of frustration in OD work.

Diagnostic Framework

Determine which parts of the OD framework require the most attention

Facilitator Note

Use this diagnostic to calibrate where the group needs to spend the most time. If foundational elements (strategy, results clarity) are weak, address those before moving to structural choices. A "stop" flag doesn't mean don't proceed—it means this area needs dedicated attention first.

Purpose

  • Assess the organization's current health across the OD model dimensions
  • Identify which areas are causing or contributing to the gaps you're experiencing
  • Prioritize where to focus in this session

1. Are the desired results clearly defined and agreed upon?

If No/Partial: Spend time in Gap Analysis before proceeding. Results must be clear before structure can be designed.

2. Is the strategy sufficiently clear to make design choices?

3. Is the issue systemic (organizational) or individual (performance)?

OD tools address systemic issues. Individual performance issues require HR/talent management approaches, not reorganization.

4. Does the current culture support or undermine the desired results?

5. Is the macro structure (overall organization shape) adequate?

6. Are micro-level processes, roles, and workflows well-designed?

7. Is there a continuous improvement mechanism in place?

Recommended Path

Personalized guidance based on your Gap Analysis, Design Criteria, Organizing Rationale, and Linkages

Facilitator Note

These recommendations update automatically as you complete the four foundational sections. Use this as your session agenda — not every section needs to be completed. Focus time where the signals indicate the highest value.

Gap Analysis

Identify the gap between current and desired results for key stakeholders

Facilitator Note

Start with RESULTS, not structure. The gap is between what we're getting and what we need. Resist the temptation to jump to solutions. Each row should represent a distinct stakeholder group's experience of the gap.

Guiding Questions

  • What results are we currently getting? What results do we need to get?
  • Who is experiencing the gap most acutely?
  • What is the magnitude and urgency of each gap?
  • What questions do we need to answer to close the gap?
Stakeholder / Group Current Results Getting Desired Results Needed The Gap
Tip Be specific about results — not activities or outputs. "We need faster decisions" is an output. "We need policy decisions made within 5 business days so clients can proceed with projects" is a result.

Stakeholder Analysis

Identify stakeholders and their requirements of the organization

Facilitator Note

Stakeholders are those who receive value FROM the organization or who the organization depends ON. Capture their specific requirements — what they need the organization to deliver. This informs design criteria.

Guiding Questions

  • Who are the key stakeholders this organization must serve?
  • What does each stakeholder group specifically need from us?
  • Which stakeholder requirements are we currently not meeting well?
  • Are there competing stakeholder requirements that create tension?
Stakeholder / Group Their Key Requirements of Us Current Performance Priority

ANCHOR / Value Offering

Clarify who you serve, what you offer, and how you deliver it

Facilitator Note

ANCHOR helps clarify the value proposition — this drives design choices. An Employee Value Offering applies the same thinking internally to attract and retain talent. Both should be explicit before structural choices are made.

ANCHOR Framework

  • A — Audience: Who are your target clients/customers?
  • N — Need: What problems or needs do you solve for them?
  • C — Channel: How do you deliver value to them?
  • H — Omit (Hold Back): Who/what are you NOT trying to serve?
  • O — Outcome/Revenue: What is the value/return for the organization?
  • R — Rationale: Why are you the right provider of this value?

Employee Value Offering

Guiding Questions

  • What do we offer employees that makes us an employer of choice?
  • What type of person thrives in this organization?
  • What are we NOT offering (and that's intentional)?

Differentiating Activities

Identify the activities that most directly deliver on your value offering

Facilitator Note

Differentiating activities are the handful of things the organization does that most directly create the value it uniquely provides. These are NOT everything the org does — they are what makes it distinctively valuable. Design should protect and enable these activities.

Guiding Questions

  • What is our core value offering to stakeholders?
  • What specific activities most directly create that value?
  • How are we uniquely doing these activities differently than others could?
  • What resources (people, technology, knowledge) does each require?
Differentiating Activity How We Do It Differently Key Resources Required
Tip Limit to 3–5 truly differentiating activities. If everything is differentiating, nothing is. These activities should drive where you invest resources and how you organize.

Design Criteria

Establish the standards any good design solution must meet

Facilitator Note

Design criteria are the decision rules — they translate strategy and stakeholder requirements into evaluable standards. Good criteria are meaningful (consequential if violated), applicable (distinguishes between options), and measurable (can be assessed). Non-negotiables are absolute requirements; all design options must meet these.

Guiding Questions

  • What must any good design solution accomplish?
  • What stakeholder requirements must the design enable?
  • What strategic outcomes must the structure support?
  • What problems must the design solve that the current structure doesn't?
Design Criterion Meaningful? Applicable? Measurable? Priority

Strategic Culture Behaviors

Define the behaviours that support and enable desired results

Facilitator Note

Culture is how we do things here. Strategic culture behaviors are the specific observable behaviors that, if widely practiced, would support achievement of desired results. The design should reinforce these behaviors through structure, metrics, rewards, and processes.

Guiding Questions

  • What behaviors, if widely demonstrated, would most support our desired results?
  • What behaviors are currently undermining our results?
  • How does our organizational design reinforce or undermine the right behaviors?
  • What structural changes would make desired behaviors easier to demonstrate?
Strategic Behavior Organizational Choice Implications
Tip Behaviors must be observable and specific — not values statements. "We collaborate across silos" is a value. "Teams proactively share project status updates with affected groups before decisions are finalized" is a behavior.

Business Model Development

Map how the organization creates and delivers value

Facilitator Note

The business model describes how the organization's work connects — inputs, key activities, outputs, and value delivered. Understanding this system is essential before deciding how to organize. Structure should follow the business model, not the other way around.

Guiding Questions

  • What are the key inputs (people, funding, information, requests) we receive?
  • What are the core processes and activities we perform to create value?
  • What outputs/services do we deliver, and to whom?
  • What are the key dependencies between our activities?
  • Where does value creation happen, and where does it break down?

Work Categorization

Classify work by its strategic value to allocate resources appropriately

Facilitator Note

This tool challenges assumptions about where time and resources go. Most organizations over-invest in Compliance and Necessary Work and under-invest in Competitive Work. The conversation about categorization is often as valuable as the output.
Competitive — Greatest Return
Enabling — Supports Competitive
Necessary — Keeps Lights On
Compliance — Required, No Return

Category Definitions

  • Competitive: Directly creates competitive advantage and strategic value. Highest investment priority.
  • Competitive Enabling: Supports and enables competitive work. Important but derivative.
  • Necessary: Keeps the organization running. Efficient delivery is the goal — not investment.
  • Compliance: Required by law/regulation. Minimize cost and effort — no strategic return.
Work Activity / Function Category % Est. Effort Should Be Notes
Tip Ask: "What would we stop doing if we had to cut 20% of effort?" Answers that feel unthinkable are often Compliance or Necessary work that has taken on false importance.

Placement of Work

Decide where work should live in the organization

Facilitator Note

Placement decisions directly drive structure. Work that varies significantly by context stays decentralized (close to the client). Work that is identical everywhere benefits from standardization or centralization. Challenge assumptions — many orgs have work in the wrong place.
Unique / Decentralized — Context-specific
Standardized — Same everywhere
Shared / Centralized — Economies of scale

Placement Decision Criteria

  • Unique/Decentralized: Work varies significantly by region/client; local expertise essential; speed of response critical
  • Standardized: Same process works everywhere; consistency is important; can be templated
  • Shared/Centralized: Economies of scale; specialized expertise; compliance/risk management; infrequent need

Organizing Rationale

Select the primary organizing logic and understand its trade-offs

Facilitator Note

Every organizing rationale has benefits AND risks — there is no perfect structure. The goal is to choose the rationale that best supports the strategy and design criteria, then proactively mitigate its inherent risks through linkage mechanisms.

Guiding Questions

  • What is the primary organizing logic that best supports our strategy?
  • What are the inherent risks of this rationale, and how will we mitigate them?
  • Do we need a secondary rationale for certain parts of the organization?

Select the primary organizing rationale:

Product / Service

Organized by what you produce
+ Deep product expertise; clear P&L; speed of delivery
- Duplication across units; poor cross-product coordination

Customer / Client

Organized by who you serve
+ Deep client knowledge; single point of contact; client focus
- Duplication of functional capability; inconsistent service standards

Function

Organized by what you do
+ Deep functional expertise; efficiency; career ladders; standards
- Siloes; slow cross-functional integration; client complexity

Process

Organized by how work flows
+ End-to-end accountability; speed; integration of steps
- Functional skills can atrophy; harder to staff; boundary management

Geography

Organized by where you operate
+ Local responsiveness; regulatory compliance; proximity to clients
- Inconsistency; duplication; limited economies of scale

Channel

Organized by how you deliver
+ Channel optimization; consistency of delivery experience
- Fragmented client experience; capability duplication

Shared / Centralized

Pooled resources and services
+ Economies of scale; consistency; specialized expertise; efficiency
- Distance from clients; slower response; service standardization tension

Dedicated / Decentralized

Embedded resources in business units
+ Proximity; responsiveness; deep client knowledge; integration
- Duplication; inconsistency; isolation; career development challenges

Virtual / Network

Networked across formal structures
+ Flexibility; cross-organizational capability; low overhead
- Accountability gaps; coordination burden; culture fragmentation

Linkages Across Organizational Boundaries

Design the mechanisms that connect work across the structure

Facilitator Note

No structure works in isolation. Linkage mechanisms connect across the silos that structure creates. Start with low-cost mechanisms and only add higher-cost ones where the coordination need is significant and persistent. Over-engineering linkages adds bureaucracy; under-engineering them creates gaps.

Guiding Questions

  • Where does work need to cross organizational boundaries?
  • What mechanisms currently exist, and are they sufficient?
  • What coordination gaps are causing problems today?
  • What is the appropriate cost/intensity of coordination for each gap?

Select mechanisms to activate (ordered low to high cost/capability):

Lowest cost — establish shared expectations and guardrails
Low cost — shared information and awareness across groups
Low cost — issues escalated to shared leader for resolution
Low cost — relationship-based coordination; relies on social capital
Moderate cost — shared platforms, workflows, or data systems
Moderate cost — shared objectives that require joint effort to achieve
Moderate cost — roles explicitly include cross-boundary coordination
Moderate-high — temporary cross-functional teams for specific outcomes
Moderate — shared behavioral norms reduce need for formal mechanisms
High — ongoing teams owning cross-boundary processes end-to-end
High — standing governance bodies for coordination and standards
High — voluntary networks to share knowledge across functions
Highest cost & capability — formal dual-authority structure; use when other mechanisms are insufficient

RACI Table

Clarify decision-making accountability across roles

Facilitator Note

RACI clarifies who does what. Each decision/activity should have exactly ONE Accountable (A). Multiple R's can work but watch for diffusion of responsibility. Too many C's and I's creates bureaucracy — be selective about who needs to be consulted vs. simply informed.

RACI Definitions

  • R — Responsible: Does the work. Executes the task.
  • A — Accountable: Ultimately answerable for the outcome. Approves. Only ONE per decision.
  • C — Consulted: Input sought before decisions are made. Two-way communication.
  • I — Informed: Notified of decisions/outcomes. One-way communication.
Tip If a row has no A, there's an accountability gap. If a column is full of R's, that role may be over-extended. If a decision has many C's, consider whether the consultation process is efficient.

Process Evaluation

Assess current processes and prioritize for redesign in the micro design phase

Facilitator Note

This bridges macro to micro design. The macro structure is set — now identify which processes within that structure are broken or misaligned and need detailed redesign. Prioritize by impact on competitive work and stakeholder value.

Guiding Questions

  • Which processes directly enable our differentiating activities?
  • Where are the biggest process breakdowns or inefficiencies?
  • Which processes cross the most organizational boundaries?
  • What is the timing for process redesign — now, next quarter, future?

Aligning Organizing Choices

Ensure all organizational systems are aligned and mutually reinforcing

Facilitator Note

This is the integration check. The Micro Design organizing systems must all point in the same direction. A misaligned system (e.g., structure says collaborate but rewards competition) will undermine the design. Use this to surface misalignments and plan interventions.

The Five Organizing Systems

  • 1. Work: What work gets done — activities, processes, volume
  • 2. Micro Structure: Roles, reporting, spans and layers, job design
  • 3. Information & Metrics: What gets measured, reported, and how decisions are made
  • 4. People & Rewards: Who does the work, capabilities needed, how they are recognized
  • 5. Continuous Improvement: How the organization learns and adapts over time

Span of Control Analysis

Determine appropriate spans and layers based on the AlignOrg framework

Facilitator Note

Span of control is how many direct reports a manager effectively oversees. There is no universal "right" number — it depends on 8 contextual factors. Rate each factor 1 (narrow span favoured) to 5 (wide span favoured). The result is a recommended range, not a rule.

How to Use

  • Rate each factor from 1 (favours a narrow span — 3-5 reports) to 5 (favours a wide span — 8-12+ reports)
  • The composite score generates a recommended span range
  • Use the notes to document decisions and exceptions

1. Strategy & Management Philosophy

1 = Centralized control, close oversight required  |  5 = Empowerment, autonomy valued
Narrow Wide 3

2. Management Capability

1 = Managers are new or developing  |  5 = Highly experienced, strong coaches
Narrow Wide 3

3. Nature of Work

1 = Complex, ambiguous, interdependent work  |  5 = Routine, well-defined, independent work
Narrow Wide 3

4. Employee Characteristics

1 = New, developing, or need close support  |  5 = Highly experienced, self-directed professionals
Narrow Wide 3

5. Dependency & Interdependence

1 = High interdependence — constant coordination across roles  |  5 = Roles are largely independent
Narrow Wide 3

6. Alignment of Goals

1 = Goal misalignment — manager must actively align team  |  5 = Strong shared purpose, self-aligning
Narrow Wide 3

7. Supporting Technologies

1 = Limited tools — high manual oversight needed  |  5 = Strong systems reduce coordination burden
Narrow Wide 3

8. Physical Location

1 = Dispersed / remote — coordination is harder  |  5 = Co-located — easy to manage and connect
Narrow Wide 3
Composite score: 3.0 — Moderate span recommended (5–7 direct reports)

Design Options Comparison

Define structural options and evaluate them against your design criteria

Facilitator Note

Generate 2-3 distinct structural options before converging. Groups that jump to one option skip creative exploration and miss better solutions. Each option should be a genuinely different organizing approach — not just minor variations of the same idea.

How to Use

  • Name each option and describe its core organizing logic
  • Score it 1–5 against each design criterion (1 = fails, 5 = excels)
  • Add qualitative notes on key trade-offs
  • The highest-scoring option against criteria is the recommended choice
